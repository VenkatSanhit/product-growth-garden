From Vision to Strategy, OKRs, Metric Trees & Experiments
 
1. From Vision to Product Strategy
1.1 Vision vs Strategy vs Roadmap (Clean Definitions)
You can think of these as three zoom levels on the same map:
•	Vision – The future world you want to create and why it matters (the “why” and the long-term “what”).
•	Strategy – The high-level choices about where you’ll play and how you’ll win to realize that vision (the “how, at a high level”).
•	Roadmap – The time‑bound plan of initiatives and bets you’ll execute to implement the strategy (the “what, when” in detail).
Multiple sources frame it this way: vision sets the destination, strategy describes the route, and the roadmap is the chronological action plan that turns strategy into concrete work.[1][2][3][4]
In practice:
•	Vision: “A world where small businesses can sell online with zero technical knowledge.”
•	Strategy: “Start with solo founders and micro‑SMBs in North America; make setup under 1 hour; win by bundling payments, hosting, and templates into one subscription; rely on self‑serve acquisition and partner channels.”
•	Roadmap: “Q3 – launch store setup wizard; Q4 – integrate payments; Q1 – release basic templates; Q2 – launch partner portal.”
The big mistake in interviews is calling a roadmap a “strategy” or reciting a feature list when they ask for strategy. Strategy is about choices and trade‑offs, not a backlog.[5][6]
1.2 The Core Components of Product Strategy
A usable product strategy answers a small set of very concrete questions:
1.	Who is this for (and not for)?
o	Target segments, jobs‑to‑be‑done, and use cases.
2.	Which problem(s) will we win on first?
o	The specific user problems you’ll own, not the entire problem space.
3.	What is our distinct approach / value prop?
o	Why users would pick us over alternatives—speed, cost, UX, integrations, ecosystem, trust, etc.[2][1]
4.	What business model supports this?
o	SaaS, usage‑based, marketplace take‑rate, ads, etc.
5.	What is our sequencing / focus over time?
o	Which segments, geos, or product surfaces we tackle first and later.
Good strategies are simple enough to remember but specific enough to exclude options. If your “strategy” doesn’t rule out some plausible paths, it isn’t a real strategy.
 
2. Strategy in Different Company Contexts
The skeleton of strategy is the same, but its expression changes by stage.
2.1 0→1 Strategy (Pre‑Product‑Market Fit)
•	Heavy on hypotheses, light on data.
•	Strategy is usually something like:
“Win over [specific early adopters] with a product that solves [hair‑on‑fire problem] better than anything else, even if it doesn’t scale yet.”
•	Focus is on learning: segment fit, problem fit, solution fit, pricing elasticity.
In interviews, for a 0→1 scenario, explicitly talk about what you’d keep flexible (pricing model, distribution channel, feature surface) and what would be non‑negotiable (problem focus, user type)—that signals you understand exploratory strategy.
2.2 1→N Strategy (Scale‑ups)
Here, product‑market fit exists. The strategic questions shift to:
•	What segments are we doubling down on vs de‑prioritizing?
•	What is our expansion motion (upmarket, new geos, adjacent use cases)?
•	How do we grow without breaking trust or the product (tech debt, UX debt, reliability)?
Strategy becomes:
“Defend our core wedge in [existing segment], expand to [adjacent segment], and build [capability] that makes us the default choice there.”
This is where you talk about platformization (e.g., turning a tool into an ecosystem or suite) and sequencing large bets across 1–3 years.[3][1]
2.3 FAANG / Big Tech Strategy
At FAANG scale:
•	Vision is stable.
•	Major constraints: existing business models, regulatory pressure, and cross‑org dependencies.
•	Strategy is often about re‑balancing:
o	Short‑term revenue vs. long‑term ecosystem health.
o	Local feature wins vs. global platform consistency.
Your strat story should show:
•	How you anchor to the company/platform vision.
•	How you align multiple partner teams.
•	How you decide when to pursue a transformational bet vs an incremental improvement.
 
3. Translating Strategy into a Roadmap
3.1 Strategy → Themes → Roadmap
A good way to avoid “feature factory” roadmaps:
1.	Start from strategy pillars (e.g., “Win mid‑market sales teams,” “Reduce time‑to‑value”).
2.	Translate pillars into outcome‑oriented themes (e.g., “Shorten onboarding to value,” “Make collaboration across regions effortless”).[7][1]
3.	Under each theme, list candidate initiatives (features, experiments, technical work).
4.	Sequence those initiatives along a timeline based on impact, effort, and dependencies—this is your roadmap.[1][3]
Unlike a backlog, a roadmap here is a story of strategic bets over time, not a laundry list.[8][3]
3.2 Outcome vs Output Roadmaps
•	Output‑based roadmap: “Q3: launch AI assistant. Q4: admin dashboard. Q1: mobile redesign.”
•	Outcome‑based roadmap: “Q3: reduce time‑to‑first‑value from 7 days to 2. Q4: increase team adoption from 2 to 4 active users per account.”
In interviews, say explicitly: “I’d frame the roadmap in terms of outcomes and tie each initiative to a specific indicator, so we can substitute the initiative if it’s not working while still committing to the outcome.”
 
4. OKRs: Connecting Strategy to Execution
4.1 What OKRs Are (and Are Not)
OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) are a way to translate strategy into time‑bound, measurable goals for a quarter or half‑year.[9][10]
•	Objective – Qualitative, inspiring, directionally aligned with strategy.
•	Key Results – 3–5 quantitative, time‑bound measures that indicate the objective has been achieved.
Example (B2B SaaS onboarding):
•	Objective: “Make new teams experience value in their first week.”
•	Key Results:
o	Increase % of new workspaces with at least 3 active members in week 1 from 35% → 55%.
o	Reduce median time‑to‑first‑key action (e.g., first project created) from 3 days → 1 day.
o	Improve trial‑to‑paid conversion from 18% → 25%.[11][9]
OKRs are not:
•	A task list (“Ship feature X”).
•	A performance‑review tool first and foremost.
•	A substitute for strategy.
They sit below strategy: strategy says what game you’re playing; OKRs say what “winning this quarter” looks like in that game.[10][9][11]
4.2 Good vs Bad OKRs
Bad examples:
•	Objective: “Grow the business.”
o	KR1: “Launch new pricing page.”
o	KR2: “Run 3 experiments.”
Problems:
•	The objective is vague.
•	KRs are activities, not outcomes. Shipping a pricing page doesn’t guarantee growth.
Better version:
•	Objective: “Increase revenue efficiency of self‑serve funnel.”
o	KR1: Increase free‑to‑paid conversion from 4% → 7%.
o	KR2: Increase ARPA (average revenue per account) from 80 → 95.
o	KR3: Reduce cost per acquisition (CPA) by 15% while maintaining conversion.[11]
Now KRs are measurable outcomes, not tasks.
4.3 Deriving OKRs from Vision, Strategy, and the North Star
Step‑by‑step:
1.	Start with vision – “We want to [future state].”
2.	Anchor in strategy – “This year, our strategic focus is [segment X, problem Y].”
3.	Connect to North Star Metric – “Our North Star is [e.g., weekly active teams] because it reflects value and predicts growth.”[12][13]
4.	Pick 1–3 leverage points in the metric tree (activation, retention, monetization).
5.	Write OKRs that move those leverage points over a quarter.
Example (Slack‑like product):
•	Vision: “Teams should spend zero time fighting tools and all their time solving problems together.”
•	North Star: “Weekly active teams (3+ members sending messages per week).”
•	Metric drivers:
o	Activation – % of new workspaces with 3+ members in week 1.
o	Engagement – Messages per active user per day.
o	Retention – 8‑week team retention.[14][15]
Q3 OKR:
•	Objective: “Help new teams become active, collaborative users within their first week.”
o	KR1: Increase % of new workspaces reaching 3+ active members in week 1 from 30% → 55%.
o	KR2: Increase median messages sent per new workspace in week 1 from 15 → 35.
o	KR3: Increase 8‑week retention for newly created workspaces from 40% → 55%.
Notice how this OKR is both:
•	Aligned to the North Star (weekly active teams).
•	Concrete enough for the team to ideate initiatives (onboarding, invitations, templates, tutorials) without locking into specific features.
 
5. Building Metric Trees: From North Star to Daily Levers
5.1 What a Metric Tree Is
A metric tree decomposes your North Star Metric into a hierarchy of drivers and sub‑drivers that your teams actually control.[16][15][14]
Think:
•	L0: North Star Metric (NSM).
•	L1: Primary input metrics that directly impact the NSM (3–5 max).
•	L2: Supporting metrics that drive each L1.
•	L3: Operational inputs teams can influence daily or weekly.
This turns your North Star from a slogan into a causal model.
5.2 Example: Consumer Subscription App (e.g., Meditation App)
North Star Metric: Weekly Active Subscribers
Breakdown:
•	L1 Drivers:
o	DAU (Daily Active Users).
o	Weekly retention (% of users who return).
o	New subscriber adds per week.
•	L2 Drivers (for DAU):
o	Notification open rate.
o	Session starts per user.
o	Content completion rate.
•	L2 Drivers (for new subscribers):
o	Free‑to‑paid conversion rate.
o	Trial start rate.
•	L3 Drivers (for notification open rate):
o	Percent of users with notifications enabled.
o	Relevance of recommendations (CTR on recommended meditation).
You can see how this tree tells you where to look when the NSM moves. Metric tree thinking is widely recommended in North Star frameworks—mixing a focus metric with structured input metrics that you can influence.[15][16][14]
5.3 Example: B2B SaaS Collaboration Tool
NSM: Weekly Active Teams (3+ members active)
•	L1:
o	New teams activated per week.
o	Team‑level retention (8‑week).
o	Team expansion (members per team).
•	L2 (New teams activated):
o	Self‑serve sign‑ups.
o	Sales‑assisted onboardings.
o	% of sign‑ups inviting 2+ teammates.
•	L2 (Retention):
o	% of teams using ≥2 core features.
o	Number of collaborative actions (comments, mentions, shared docs).
•	L3:
o	“Invite teammate” CTA click rate.
o	Share flows performance (email/UI).
o	Number of templates used in first week.
Once you have this, team‑level metrics become obvious:
•	Growth owns sign‑ups, trial → team activation.
•	Product onboarding team owns first‑week behavior.
•	Core product teams own multi‑feature engagement.
5.4 How Metric Trees Bridge Strategy → Experiments
Metric trees let you:
•	Ensure every team metric rolls up to the NSM.
•	Identify bottlenecks when the NSM underperforms.
•	Give teams autonomy: “You own L2 metric X; as long as you move it and respect guardrails, you’re free to experiment.”[16][14][15]
In interviews, being able to sketch a rough metric tree on a whiteboard is a huge signal of seniority.
 
6. Experiments: Turning Metrics into Learning Loops
6.1 The Experiment Loop
To move a metric, you design hypothesis‑driven experiments:
1.	Identify a lever in the metric tree (e.g., “trial→activation”).
2.	Articulate a hypothesis:
“If we [change X], then [metric Y] will improve because [user behavior insight].”
3.	Design the experiment:
o	Variant(s), control, population, duration.
o	Decide success metrics (primary) and guardrails (secondary).
4.	Run, measure, decide:
o	Did the primary metric improve, with acceptable impact on guardrails?
5.	Ship or discard:
o	If positive and practical, roll out. If not, learn and move on.
This is how you connect strategy → metric tree → experiments into a repeatable operating system.[17][15]
6.2 Leading vs Lagging Indicators in Experiments
You rarely run experiments directly on the North Star because:
•	NSM is often slow to react.
•	NSM is influenced by many factors.
Instead, you use leading indicators—metrics that move faster and predict the NSM.[18][19]
Example:
•	NSM: Weekly Active Teams.
•	Leading indicator: % of new workspaces with 3+ members in week 1.
Experiment:
•	Hypothesis: “If we auto‑prompt workspace creators to invite teammates at the end of onboarding, we’ll increase the % of workspaces with 3+ members in week 1, which will improve weekly active teams.”
You measure the leading indicator during the experiment; you validate impact on the NSM over a longer period.
In interviews, explicitly distinguishing leading vs lagging indicators is a good way to show you understand experimentation rigor.[20][18]
6.3 Guardrail Metrics
Guardrail metrics ensure you don’t “win the metric, lose the product.”
Examples:
•	For engagement experiments:
o	Guardrail: user satisfaction, complaint volume, churn.
•	For monetization experiments:
o	Guardrail: NPS, refund requests, negative social sentiment.
A common pattern: North Star (or driver) as the primary, plus 1–2 guardrails that capture trust, quality, and user experience.[15][17]
 
7. Handling Trade‑offs & Constraints Using Vision + NSM + OKRs
This is where everything ties together.
7.1 Speed vs Quality
Scenario: Sales is pushing for more features; engineering is drowning; quality is dropping.
How you can structure this:
•	Vision lens: “Will shipping more rough features actually move us toward our vision of [trusted, reliable X]?”
•	NSM lens: “Our NSM is weekly active teams. Shipping unstable features may boost short‑term sign‑ups but hurt retention, which is a key driver in our metric tree.”
•	OKR lens: “Our Q3 objective is to improve retention. Quality regressions directly conflict with that.”
Then propose: “We should define an OKR around reliability or user‑reported issues as a guardrail, and sequence fewer, higher‑impact releases this quarter.”
7.2 Growth vs User Trust
Scenario: Growth team wants aggressive notifications; trust & safety is worried.
•	Vision: Are we building a product people love using, or one that simply maximizes attention?
•	NSM: If our NSM is time spent in the app, we might be tempted to spam. But if our vision is about healthy improvement (e.g., fitness, mental health), we should consider an NSM that better reflects that (e.g., “weekly active users completing 3+ meaningful sessions”).
•	Metric tree: Add a guardrail for opt‑out rates or notification disablement.
In interviews, showing this values lens plus metric lens makes you sound like a senior PM, not just a growth hacker.
7.3 Tech Debt vs New Features
Scenario: Engineering wants to refactor; product wants to deliver net new.
Position it as:
•	“Our strategy requires shipping [capability] in 12 months. Our current architecture can’t support [scale/latency/complexity]. By not paying tech debt now, we risk blocking the strategy.”
•	Use OKRs:
o	Objective: “Make the platform ready for [future bet].”
o	KRs: “Reduce p95 latency by X,” “Increase test coverage to Y,” etc.
This keeps tech work connected to strategy, not just “engineering asks.”
 
8. Interview Mastery: How to Talk About Strategy, OKRs, and Metrics
Here’s how to apply all this in real interviews.
8.1 Product Design / Strategy Questions
When they ask: “Design a product for X,” or “What’s your strategy for Y?” structure your answer roughly like this:
1.	Clarify the vision & success state.
o	“What would success look like in 3–5 years for [this product]?”
2.	Define target users and problems.
o	Who is this for, what’s broken for them.
3.	Propose strategic pillars (choices).
o	“We’ll focus on [segment A], win by [differentiator], and monetize via [model].”
4.	Propose a North Star Metric and rationale.
o	“I’d pick [metric] because it reflects [user value] and predicts [business outcome].”[13][12]
5.	Sketch a metric tree.
o	“This would be driven by activation, engagement, and retention; at team level that becomes X, Y, Z.”[14][15]
6.	Describe a 3–6 month roadmap in themes.
o	“First, solve activation; second, deepen engagement; third, build share/export features.”
7.	Talk about experiments.
o	“We’d test hypotheses like…, measure using [leading indicator], while watching [guardrails].”
That shows you think end‑to‑end from vision → strategy → metrics → roadmap → experiments.
8.2 “Set OKRs for This Product”
Use this pattern:
1.	Anchor in vision and strategy.
2.	Re‑state or propose a North Star.
3.	Pick 1–2 critical drivers from the metric tree.
4.	Write one objective per driver with 2–3 measurable KRs.
Example:
•	NSM: Weekly active sellers on a marketplace.
•	Driver: Seller activation & retention.
OKR:
•	Objective: “Make the marketplace the default place sellers list inventory.”
o	KR1: Increase monthly active sellers from 1k → 1.5k.
o	KR2: Increase median listings per active seller from 10 → 15.
o	KR3: Reduce monthly seller churn from 8% → 5%.[10][11]
Then mention initiatives you’d explore, but always keep them subordinate to the OKRs.
8.3 “Tell Me About a Time You Made a Trade‑off”
Use a STAR‑like narrative but highlight:
•	Vision & NSM context – what were you trying to optimize?
•	Metrics and data – what was the metric tree showing?
•	Stakeholder perspectives – what did eng, sales, leadership want?
•	Your decision – which levers you prioritized and why.
•	Result – what happened to key metrics and what you learned.
This demonstrates real‑world application of everything in Volumes 1 and 2.
 
Quick Mental Model Summary (Volume 2)
•	Vision – The “why” and long‑term “what.”
•	Strategy – Coherent choices about where to play and how to win, derived from vision.
•	Roadmap – Time‑bound bets and initiatives to implement the strategy.
•	North Star Metric – Quantitative reflection of core user value that predicts business success.
•	Metric Tree – Decomposition of the NSM into drivers and operational inputs teams control.[16][14][15]
•	OKRs – Quarterly goals that move specific drivers in the metric tree, aligned with strategy.[9][10][11]
•	Experiments – Hypothesis‑driven changes aimed at moving leading indicators while respecting guardrails.[17][18]
Tie these together in interview answers and you’ll sound like someone who can actually run a product, not just talk about features.
 
Pocket Interview Checklist (Volume 2)
Before you answer any strategy/metrics/OKR question, mentally run through:
1.	Have I stated or clarified the vision and success state?
2.	Have I defined target user and core problems?
3.	Have I articulated clear strategic choices and trade‑offs?
4.	Did I propose a North Star Metric and justify why?[12][13]
5.	Can I sketch a simple metric tree (NSM → drivers → team metrics)?[14][15]
6.	Did I define outcomes (OKRs), not just features?[9][10]
7.	Have I mentioned how I’d measure and experiment (leading indicators + guardrails)?[18][17]
8.	Did I acknowledge constraints (tech, org, market) and show how I’d handle trade‑offs?
If you can hit those consistently, you’re operating at a senior‑PM / group‑PM level in interviews.
